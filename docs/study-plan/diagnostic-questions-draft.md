# Diagnostic — Full Question Draft (for editing)

Every question with real content, the correct answer, and what a mistake reveals.
"Reveals" = the level ceiling that mistake sets (and the lesson it points to).

---

## SECTION 1 — Listen & Type (Dictation) — 2 sentences

Student hears the sentence and types it. We check 3 things: words/spelling, grammar, commas & capitals.

### Q1 (easier)
**Audio says:** "When the meeting ended, the students returned to the library to finish their group project."

| If they get wrong... | What it means |
|---|---|
| Word order / missing words | most basic problem → **below 80** |
| "library", "project", "returned" misspelled | spelling/vocab → **below 100** |
| "ended" → "end", "returned" → "return" (no past tense) | past tense weak → **100–110** |
| Forgets comma after "When the meeting ended" | commas only → **about 105–110** |
| All correct | clean → **120+** |

### Q2 (harder)
**Audio says:** "Although the experiment was repeated several times, the researchers could not explain the unexpected results."

| If they get wrong... | What it means |
|---|---|
| Sentence falls apart / words missing | **below 80** |
| "experiment", "researchers", "unexpected" misspelled | **below 100** |
| "was repeated" → "repeated" / "was repeat" | passive voice weak → **110–120** |
| Forgets comma after "Although … times" | commas only → **about 105–110** |
| All correct | clean → **120+** |

## SECTION 2 — Fill in ONE word — 12 blanks, ONE connected story

All blanks come from the **same story** (B1–C1 vocabulary). Student types **one word only**
— for passive/perfect, the helper word (was / has / are) is already printed, so they only
write the verb form. The **first level that breaks** sets the ceiling.

**The story (blanks numbered):**

> Nina firmly **(1)** ____ (believe) that effort matters more than talent. Every morning she
> **(2)** ____ (wake) up before dawn and **(3)** ____ (devote) two hours to her English.
>
> Last year, however, she nearly gave up. She **(4)** ____ (fail) her first practice exam, and
> the disappointing result **(5)** ____ (leave) her feeling hopeless. For weeks she **(6)** ____
> (struggle) to find any motivation at all.
>
> Then her mentor offered advice that was carefully **(7)** ____ (choose). Since that day, Nina
> has **(8)** ____ (overcome) one obstacle after another, and her essays are now **(9)** ____
> (write) with remarkable confidence.
>
> **(10)** ____ (review) a little every single day became her secret. If she **(11)** ____ (be)
> honest, she would admit it was never easy — but her mentor always insisted that she **(12)**
> ____ (be) patient with herself.

### Answers + what each level reveals

| # | Verb given | Answer (one word) | Level — wrong here means |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | believe | **believes** | Present simple (he/she/it + s) → **below 100** |
| 2 | wake | **wakes** | Present simple → **below 100** |
| 3 | devote | **devotes** | Present simple → **below 100** |
| 4 | fail | **failed** | Past tense → **100–110** |
| 5 | leave | **left** | Past (irregular) → **100–110** |
| 6 | struggle | **struggled** | Past tense → **100–110** |
| 7 | choose | **chosen** | Passive (helper "was" given) → **110–120** |
| 8 | overcome | **overcome** | Perfect (helper "has" given) → **110–120** |
| 9 | write | **written** | Passive (helper "are" given) → **110–120** |
| 10 | review | **Reviewing** | Gerund as subject → **120–135** |
| 11 | be | **were** | Unreal conditional ("If she were…") → **120–135** |
| 12 | be | **be** | Subjunctive ("insisted that she be") → **120–135** |

Notes:
- #7–#9 stay one word because the helper word is printed in the story — the student only
  supplies the participle. No two-word answers anywhere.
- #11: only **were** = full credit. "was" is common in casual speech, so accept it but treat it
  as **not** reaching the top level (don't clear the 120–135 gate).
- "review" replaces "practise" because practise/practice differs UK vs US — review is the same
  in both.

## SECTION 4 — Read a Passage + Answer (4 questions)

Tests if they **understand the meaning**, not just know words. If word-spotting (Section 3) is
high but they miss these → the problem is **reading skill**, not vocabulary.

**Passage:**
> Many city governments are now encouraging people to use bicycles instead of cars. Bicycles
> do not produce pollution, they are cheaper to own, and they help people stay healthy. However,
> some people argue that cities are not safe enough for cycling. Without protected bike lanes,
> riders must share the road with fast-moving traffic. Supporters reply that the solution is not
> to give up, but to build better roads designed for everyone.

| # | Question | Answer | What it tests |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Best title? (a) The Dangers of Cars (b) Why Cities Should Support Cycling (c) How to Fix a Bicycle | **(b)** | main idea |
| 2 | Which is **NOT** a reason the passage gives for cycling? (a) no pollution (b) cheaper to own (c) faster than cars (d) better for health | **(c)** | finding details |
| 3 | What do supporters say is the solution to the safety problem? (a) stop cycling in cities (b) build better roads & protected lanes (c) ride more slowly (d) buy safer bikes | **(b)** | inference |
| 4 | "share the road" here means…? (a) give the road away (b) use the road at the same time as cars (c) repair the road | **(b)** | word meaning in context |

> All four are multiple-choice on purpose — open text can't be graded consistently (a one-word
> typo or a synonym would be marked wrong). Every reading answer is a tap, not typing.

## SECTION 5 — Speak / Reply (Conversation) — record answer

Student records a spoken answer. We check: do they **answer the question**, speak **smoothly**,
and is the **grammar correct while talking** (grammar that's fine on paper often breaks in speech).

**Prompt shown:**
> "Tell us about a place you would like to visit and explain why. You have 30 seconds to prepare
> and 60 seconds to speak."

| We listen for | Simple meaning |
|---|---|
| Did they answer the question? | on-topic, complete answer |
| Smoothness | not too many long pauses / restarts |
| Grammar while speaking | tenses & verb forms hold up in speech |
| Word range | uses more than the most basic words |
| Clear to understand | a listener can follow easily |

The grammar mistakes heard here also pull down the writing level (someone who says "he go"
can't write at 120).

## ACCURACY RULES — close the loopholes

These are the holes that make the score lie. Each has a fix.

| Loophole | Why it breaks accuracy | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Phone autocorrect / spellcheck** on typed answers | The phone silently fixes spelling & grammar → dictation and spelling scores become fake | Turn **off** autocorrect, autocomplete, and the red spell-underline on every typing box (Sections 1, 2). The spelling section (3) is tap-only, so no typing to fix. |
| **Copy–paste / Google lookup** | Student pastes the correct sentence or looks up a word | Disable paste on the typing boxes; one prompt on screen at a time |
| **British vs American spelling** | A US-spelling student marks a correct UK word as "wrong" (e.g. *practise*, *acknowledgement*, *colour*) and loses points unfairly | Already removed those words from the lists. For Sections 1–2, **accept both** spellings as correct (e.g. *organise/organize*) |
| **Contractions & periods in dictation** | "could not" vs "couldn't", or a missing full stop, counted as an error | Accept both forms; ignore the final period; only the **graded comma** counts |
| **Capital letters** | Auto-capitalize at the start of a line marks lowercase answers wrong | Grade **case-insensitive**, except where a capital is the thing being tested |
| **"Mark everything wrong" in spelling** | Student flags all 40 → gets the 20 wrong ones "right" by luck | Already handled: score = correct catches **minus** false alarms (flagging a correctly-spelled word). Flag-all = zero |
| **Re-test = memorizing the same questions** | Paid users re-take to show "improvement", but it's the **same set** → fake gain | Build **2–3 parallel versions** of each section. Re-test serves a different version. *(Same risk exists today in the fixed 20-step mock and the 9-step mini-diagnosis — they reuse one set.)* |
| **Speaking: reading a script / using AI** | Pre-written or AI answer isn't their real speaking | Short prep time, a fresh prompt each attempt, and the grader flags unnaturally perfect/over-long answers |
| **Time limits only on screen** | Student edits the clock or reloads to get more time | Enforce the timer on the server, not just the browser |
